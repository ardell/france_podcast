How to Drink France — A Personal Wine Companion

France (wine, thematic) · Tuesday, July 14, 2026 · 08:24

There is one small thing that trips up nearly every American in front of a French wine list, and once you see it, the whole country opens up like a door. Back home, a label tells you the grape. You buy a Cabernet, a Pinot Noir, a Chardonnay; the grape is the brand, the star, the name on the marquee. In France, the label almost never tells you the grape. It tells you the place. A bottle says Bandol, or Morgon, or Pouilly-Fuissé, and it assumes you already know what grows there and what it tastes like. This is not the French being difficult, though it can feel that way at eight in the evening with a tired waiter hovering. It is the whole philosophy of terroir made into law, the same idea we keep circling all week, the belief that a specific patch of ground can be tasted, and that the ground, not the grape, is the thing worth naming. The upshot is wonderfully practical: you do not need to memorize French wine. You need to know a handful of places. Learn five or six names and where they are on the map, and you can walk into any restaurant from Paris to Lyon and order exactly the wine you love. So let me hand you those names, tailored to your palate, the smooth earthy big reds and the round buttery whites you told me you chase.

Start with the reds, because that is where your heart is. You already know the Rhône is your homeland for big, dark, peppery, earthy wine, and we have a whole conversation coming about that river and its Syrah and Grenache, so I will not repeat it here; when someone hands you a Rhône list, you are covered. What I want to add is the region Americans almost never think to reach for, and it may be the truest match of all for the words you used, big and earthy and smooth. It is down here in Provence, on the coast near Toulon, and it is called Bandol. Bandol red is built on a thick-skinned, sun-loving grape called Mourvèdre, which the appellation requires to be at least half the blend and often much more, sometimes nearly all of it. Mourvèdre is dark and tannic and a little wild; people reach for words like leather, garrigue, wild herb, warm earth, even a whiff of the animal, of the barnyard, in the best sense. By law Bandol reds are aged at least eighteen months in big old wooden casks, which rounds those fierce tannins into something smooth, and the wine is famously long-lived; a serious Bandol can want ten years before it truly opens and can drink beautifully at fifteen or twenty. If you see it on a list, order it, especially with red meat. That is your big earthy red, hiding in plain sight in rosé country.

Then, as the cruise ends in Lyon, look just north of the city for a wine I want to rescue from its own reputation, because most Americans think they already know it and they are wrong. Beaujolais. In your mind it may be that thin, purple, candy-ish stuff called Beaujolais Nouveau, rushed to market every November in a marketing blitz, meant to be drunk cold and forgotten by Christmas. Set that aside entirely. Above the everyday wine sit ten small villages, the crus of Beaujolais, and their wines are a different creature. They are still made from the Gamay grape, but from old vines on granite hillsides, made the serious way and often given time in oak, and they can be deep, earthy, structured, and smooth in exactly the way you like. Learn three names: Morgon, whose wines from the slope called the Côte du Py are dense and long-lived; Moulin-à-Vent, the firmest and most age-worthy, with real grip and dark fruit; and Fleurie, the perfumed and silky one. A good Morgon or Moulin-à-Vent can age a decade and, as it does, it starts to smell uncannily like fine red Burgundy at a fraction of the price. This is the local wine as you step off the boat. Drink it where it lives.

Now the whites, and here I get to explain the word you used, buttery, because you are a cook and you will want to know the why. When a white wine tastes buttery, that flavor is usually not the oak, though oak helps. It is chemistry. Most wines go through a second, quiet fermentation called malolactic fermentation, where bacteria convert the sharp malic acid, the crisp acid of a green apple, into softer lactic acid, the acid of milk and cream. And that conversion throws off a compound called diacetyl, which is, quite literally, the molecule the food industry uses to make things taste like butter. It is the butter flavor, the same one on your movie popcorn. Round that out with time in oak barrels and time resting on the spent yeast, and you get the sensation you love, rich and creamy and broad. Now, where does France do this best and most classically? In white Burgundy, which is to say Chardonnay, though the label will only ever say the place. And the good news is that the southern end of Burgundy sits right above Beaujolais, a short drive from Lyon: the Mâconnais, and its jewel, Pouilly-Fuissé, all Chardonnay, rich and rounded and golden. Ask for a Mâcon or a Pouilly-Fuissé and you will have your buttery white. Reach further north toward the Côte de Beaune, to names like Meursault, and it becomes glorious and expensive; there the French tend to use that buttery technique with restraint, more silk than custard, but the family resemblance is right there in the glass. And for a round, textured white without the butter, the white Rhône we will talk about elsewhere does the same warm, waxy trick with different grapes.

Which leaves the pink wine you will see everywhere on the southern half of this trip, so pale it is nearly silver: Provence rosé. This is not sweet blush wine. It is bone dry, delicate, made mostly from Grenache, Cinsault, and Syrah, pressed pale on purpose, and it has become one of the great American love stories in wine. Imports from Provence exploded in this century, the United States is now its single biggest foreign market, and for a while it seemed every summer terrace at home ran on hashtag rosé all day. Interpretation, not fact, but I think it caught on precisely because it is so easy, so unpretentious, so at home with food and sunshine.

Let me leave you the food, because your kitchen is Mediterranean and this all points straight at your table. The big earthy reds, Bandol and the Rhône, want lamb, want grilled and herb-rubbed meat, want a slow beef daube. The buttery white Burgundy wants a cream sauce, a roast chicken, a river fish. The Provence rosé wants everything summery and garlicky, tapenade, aïoli, ratatouille, a salade niçoise. And the cru Beaujolais wants the food of Lyon itself, charcuterie and the hearty bouchon cooking you will meet at the end.

So here is the heuristic, the thing to carry in your pocket. Find the place-name on the list, place it roughly on the map, and let geography tell you the style: south and warm means big reds and pale rosé, up near Lyon means serious Gamay, just above that means buttery Chardonnay. And if all else fails, tell the waiter the truth in plain words: you like smooth, earthy, big reds and round, balanced whites. Taste is subjective, and my butter explanation is real chemistry but simplified, so trust your own mouth in the end. But that one sentence, in French wine country, is a passport. Hand it over, and the whole country pours you a glass.
