The Restaurant Was Born Here — Paris on a Plate

Paris (food) · Monday, July 6, 2026 · 08:49

Let me start with a claim that sounds like tourist-board puffery but happens to be more or less true: the restaurant, as an institution, was invented in Paris. Not cooking, obviously, and not eating out, which is as old as cities. But the specific thing you did last night, walked into a room, were handed a list of dishes each with its own price, sat at your own small table at whatever hour suited you, and chose for yourself from among the options, that particular ritual was assembled here in the last decades before the Revolution. And the story of how it happened is the story of this whole city's food, because it turns on the same hinge everything in Paris turns on: the fall of the aristocracy.

There is a founding legend you will hear, about a man named Boulanger who around seventeen sixty-five hung out a sign advertising restorants, restoratives, meaning nourishing broths meant to restore your health, and that the word restaurant comes from that soup. I have to flag this one, because the historian Rebecca Spang went looking for Monsieur Boulanger and could not actually find solid evidence he existed. The word is real, the restorative broths are real, but the tidy origin story is probably a myth that snowballed over two centuries. What is better documented is a place called the Grande Taverne de Londres, opened in the early seventeen eighties by a man named Beauvilliers, who put together the whole package we now take for granted: the menu, the individual tables, the fixed hours, the wine list, a proper dining room. That is the template. Everything since is variation.

But the thing that turned a handful of these places into an explosion was the Revolution, the same upheaval we have been tracing through these Paris walks. Think about the mechanics of it. Before seventeen eighty-nine, the best cooking in France happened in private, behind the walls of aristocratic houses, where a great noble family employed a brigade of cooks to feed itself and impress its guests. That talent was locked up, invisible, serving maybe a few dozen privileged mouths. Then the Revolution came, the aristocracy fled or was killed, and suddenly all those brilliant, highly trained chefs were unemployed, their masters gone. What does a cook do when the count has lost his head? He opens a restaurant. He takes the refined cooking that had been the private privilege of the nobility and he sells it, by the plate, to anyone with the money to pay, and the new customers were the rising bourgeoisie, the merchants and lawyers and officials of the new order. By eighteen oh-four, Paris had something like five hundred restaurants. This is one of my favorite examples of a theme we keep hitting, that the grand political drama and the everyday fabric of life are the same story told at different scales. The Revolution was about where authority comes from, about the fall of a ruling class, and one of its most durable products turned out to be dinner. The aristocracy's cuisine escaped the château and became public property. In a sense the food was democratized before almost anything else was.

And then the profession got its theorists, its geniuses. Antonin Carême, working in the early eighteen hundreds, took cooking and self-consciously made it an art, with grand architectural constructions and a codified system of sauces, a man who cooked for kings and emperors and diplomats and treated the kitchen as a serious intellectual discipline. Later in the century Auguste Escoffier streamlined and modernized all of it, organized the professional kitchen into the brigade system that restaurants still use today, the line of specialized stations, and cemented French technique as the grammar that Western fine dining would speak for the next hundred years. This is why, when you go to culinary school almost anywhere in the world, you still learn the terms in French. Paris did not just produce good food. It produced the operating system.

Now let me get to the actual eating, because the ideas are only interesting if they end in your mouth. Start with bread, because the French start with bread. The baguette you tear into at breakfast is more recent and more regulated than you would guess. There is an actual law, the bread decree of nineteen ninety-three, that defines what may be called a baguette de tradition française: flour, water, salt, and yeast or leaven, and nothing else, no freezing of the dough, no additives, made on the premises. It is worth pausing on how French that is, that the state would legislate the definition of good bread, the same centralizing instinct that gave you the metric system now guarding the honor of a loaf. A real tradition baguette goes stale within a day, which is why Parisians buy bread constantly, often twice a day, and why the walk to the boulangerie is one of the load-bearing rituals of ordinary life here. The croissant beside it, I should confess, is not originally French at all. It descends from the Austrian kipferl, brought to Paris by a Viennese baker in the eighteen thirties, and only later did French bakers rebuild it with laminated butter dough, folding butter into the dough again and again to make those shattering layers. The French did to the croissant what they did to the aristocracy's cooking, took something and perfected it into something unmistakably their own.

Then the pastry, the pâtisserie, which in Paris is treated with the seriousness other cultures reserve for jewelry. A great Parisian pastry shop is a vitrine of small architectural objects, the glazed fruit tarts with their geometric rings of fruit, the tarte Tatin with its caramelized apples baked upside down, the éclairs and the mille-feuille with its thousand leaves of pastry and cream. And the dessert I would send you toward, because you can make it yourself and because its history is a nice puzzle, is crème brûlée. The earliest known recipe for that burnt-sugar custard appears in a French cookbook by François Massialot in sixteen ninety-one, where he describes taking a fire-reddened iron to caramelize the top, though the English and the Catalans both claim their own versions, so the origin is genuinely contested and I would not swear the French invented it, only that they perfected the marketing. Crack that glass lid of caramel with your spoon and you are performing a technique three and a half centuries old.

For the savory heart of Parisian eating, though, forget the fancy and think bistro. The bistro is where the city actually eats, and its dishes are simple and repeated and beloved. Steak frites, a seared steak and a pile of proper fries, is practically the national plate of everyday Paris, the thing you order when you do not want to think. And because Paris sits at the end of the rivers and the rail lines that bring the sea to the city, the seafood is extraordinary despite there being no coast for miles. A Parisian brasserie in the cold months sets out great tiered platters of oysters and shellfish, and yes, the razor clams you mentioned, the couteaux, long and slender in their shells, quickly cooked with garlic and parsley and a hit of white wine. This is where your Mediterranean cooking instincts will feel at home, the same garlic and parsley and olive oil logic, just aimed at the cold-water shellfish of the Atlantic.

And this brings me to the dining culture itself, which is the real subject. In Paris a meal is not fuel and it is not a transaction to be completed quickly, which is the thing that most surprises Americans and sometimes annoys us. The table is held for you. The waiter will not bring the check until you ask, because to rush you would be an insult. Lunch can take two hours. The meal has a shape, a sequence, a rhythm, and the point is the sitting there as much as the eating. It is the café conversation we talked about, moved to the table and given three courses. And when you set it against the American way, the speed, the to-go cup, the check dropped while you are still chewing, you can see two different philosophies of time and pleasure. The French built a civilization in which the daily meal is treated as one of the things worth taking seriously, worth protecting, even worth writing into law. Kings fell and republics rose and the whole political order was torn down and rebuilt more than once, but the Parisian sat down to lunch through all of it, and is sitting down to it right now. That, in the end, is what endures.
