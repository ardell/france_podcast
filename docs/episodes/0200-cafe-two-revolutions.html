The Café and the Two Revolutions

Paris, Saint-Germain-des-Prés · Monday, July 6, 2026 · 08:46

You have been walking Saint-Germain-des-Prés, so let me send you down one particular street, the rue de l'Ancienne Comédie, a narrow lane just off the Boulevard Saint-Germain. Halfway along it there is a restaurant with a red awning and gold lettering that calls itself Le Procope, and it makes a large claim about itself: the oldest café in Paris, opened in sixteen eighty-six. Now, be a little careful with that word oldest, because these superlatives get slippery. The room you would walk into today is a restaurant that opened in nineteen fifty-seven, in a space that had been many things after the original café closed in the eighteen seventies. So it is not one unbroken candle burning since the seventeenth century. But the founding date is real. A Sicilian named Francesco Procopio dei Coltelli, who took the French name François Procope, opened a coffee house here, and he sold something new and thrilling to Parisians, coffee, and also, being Sicilian, ices, sorbets and flavored frozen creams, the ancestors of gelato, served in a proper room where you could sit and talk. Hold that detail, because it matters more than it looks. An Italian brought the frozen dessert and the coffee culture north, and around them grew a room for conversation. Food and drink came first. The ideas came because there was finally somewhere comfortable to sit and argue.

And argue they did. This is what I want you to feel as you stand on that street. The café and its cousin the salon, the private drawing room where a hostess gathered clever people around her, were the physical hardware of the Enlightenment. You cannot have a movement of ideas without somewhere for the ideas to collide. In an age with no talk radio and no internet, the café was the machine that let a lawyer, a printer, a bankrupt aristocrat, and a visiting foreigner sit at the same marble table and take each other's arguments seriously. Le Procope became legendary for exactly this. The stories say Voltaire drank prodigious quantities of coffee here, that the young Encyclopédistes, the men building Diderot's great Encyclopedia, that audacious attempt to put all human knowledge between covers, gathered in these rooms. I will be honest with you: some of those specific anecdotes are the kind of legend that accretes around a famous place, and a careful historian would raise an eyebrow at the more colorful ones. But the underlying truth is not in doubt. This quarter, the Left Bank around Saint-Germain, was the beating heart of a conversation about a genuinely radical question. Where does legitimate authority actually come from? For a thousand years the answer had been settled: from God, through the king. The Enlightenment dared to answer differently. From reason. From the consent of the governed. From rights that belong to a person simply for being a person.

Now here is where your American mind should start to prickle, because that question was about to get two very different answers, and one of them was being worked out by your own countrymen, some of them right here in Paris.

Into this world, in seventeen seventy-six, sailed Benjamin Franklin. He was seventy years old, already world-famous as the man who had tamed lightning, and he came as America's envoy to beg, borrow, and charm France into backing the Revolution. He settled not in the center of the city but in Passy, then a leafy village on the western edge of Paris, in a house lent to him by a wealthy French supporter. And Franklin did something brilliant. He understood that these Parisians, drunk on Enlightenment ideas about natural man and simple virtue, were desperate to believe in exactly the kind of figure he could pretend to be. So he put on a plain brown coat and a fur cap instead of a wig, and he let the most sophisticated society on earth decide that he was a noble savage from the forests of Pennsylvania, a philosopher in homespun. It was theater, and it worked. His face went onto medallions and rings and snuffboxes. Fashionable women wore their hair in a style called the coiffure à la Franklin. He was, and I do not think this is an exaggeration, the first American celebrity in Europe. And that celebrity was a weapon. It helped him land the French alliance of seventeen seventy-eight that arguably won the war, and later helped seal the Treaty of Paris in seventeen eighty-three that ended it.

Then, in seventeen eighty-five, Franklin went home, and a lanky, red-haired Virginian arrived to take his place as American minister to France. Thomas Jefferson. There is a lovely exchange, well documented, when the French foreign minister said to him, "You replace Monsieur Franklin, I hear." And Jefferson answered, "I succeed. No man can replace him." Jefferson served here from seventeen eighty-five until the autumn of seventeen eighty-nine, and that timing is the whole point of this episode, because it means the author of the American Declaration of Independence was living in Paris, walking these same streets, at the precise moment France began to come apart.

And the man who ties the two revolutions together with a single ribbon is the Marquis de Lafayette. Lafayette was a French aristocrat who had sailed to America as a very young man, fought alongside Washington, and come home a hero of the American cause. So when France's own crisis broke open in seventeen eighty-nine, here was a man who had physically lived the American experiment and now wanted to bring it home. In the summer of that year Lafayette drafted a founding document for the new France, the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen. And here is the connection that should give you chills as you stand in Saint-Germain: Lafayette consulted Jefferson while drafting it. The record is clear on this. Lafayette sent Jefferson his draft, asked him to make his observations, even asked to borrow Jefferson's notes on the American Bill of Rights. Jefferson annotated it, suggested cuts. Now, I want to flag the limit of that claim, because it is often overstated. Jefferson did not write the French declaration; the final adopted text of August seventeen eighty-nine drew heavily on other French thinkers, especially the Abbé Sieyès. But the transatlantic conversation was real. The ideas that had been argued out in these cafés, then carried across the ocean and written into American law, were now being carried back and rewritten in French.

So you have two revolutions, born of the same Enlightenment question, in dialogue with each other through the same handful of men. And here is the thing that I think about every time I picture Jefferson in Paris in seventeen eighty-nine. He sailed home that autumn believing he had witnessed the birth of an American-style republic in France. He was catastrophically wrong. Within four years the French Revolution had devoured its moderates, sent Lafayette fleeing for his life, and produced the Terror, the guillotine working daily just across the river from where you have been walking. The American Revolution produced a stable republic that endures. The French produced the Terror, then Napoleon, then a century of revolutions and restorations before the thing finally settled. Historians have argued for two hundred years about why, and I will not pretend to resolve it. But the way I read it, the difference was partly this: the Americans were building something new on relatively open ground, and the French were trying to demolish a thousand-year-old structure of king and Church and aristocracy while standing inside it. When you tear down the whole house of legitimacy at once, you had better have somewhere to stand.

And now walk back out onto the rue de l'Ancienne Comédie, and notice what is still here. The café. Not that exact room, but the institution. People are sitting at little tables with a coffee and something sweet, arguing, watching the street, exactly as they were doing when Franklin was in Passy and the world was about to turn over. The kings fell. The Terror came and went. Napoleon rose and was exiled. But the coffee still comes, and the ices the Sicilian brought north are still on the menu down the street. That is the quiet lesson of Saint-Germain. Ideas can burn a world down. Everyday life, the cup and the conversation, somehow outlasts them all.
