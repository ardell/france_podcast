What the Revolution Actually Changed

France (thematic) · Monday, July 6, 2026 · 08:19

When we say "the French Revolution," a certain picture arrives almost automatically. A crowd. A blade. A king kneeling on a scaffold in what is now the Place de la Concorde, a few minutes' walk from wherever you are standing right now. And that did happen — Louis the Sixteenth lost his head in January of seventeen ninety-three, and his queen followed that October. But I want to make a case to you today that the killing of the king, dramatic as it was, is almost the least interesting thing the Revolution did. Beheading a monarch is old news. The English did it to Charles the First a century and a half earlier. What the French attempted was stranger and far more ambitious. They tried to rationalize a nation. They tried to take the whole tangled, inherited, thousand-year accumulation of local custom and measure and loyalty, and sweep it away, and replace it with something clean, uniform, and administered from the center. That project — not the guillotine — is the thing whose consequences you are still walking through today.

Start with something you would never think to notice: the meter. Before the Revolution, France did not have a system of measurement. It had thousands of them. The historians' figure that gets repeated is that there were something like a quarter of a million different local units of weight and measure in use across the kingdom, and I will flag that as the kind of number that is impressive precisely because it is hard to pin down — but the underlying reality is not disputed. A "pound" in one town was not a "pound" in the next. A measure of grain meant one thing under this lord and another thing twenty miles down the road. This suited the old order fine, because the man who controls the size of the bushel controls the size of the rent. Local measure was local power.

So in seventeen ninety, the new National Assembly asked the Academy of Sciences to fix it — to build one measure, for all people, for all time. And here is the part that tells you everything about the revolutionary mind. They did not want the new unit to come from a king's forearm or any human authority at all. They wanted it drawn from the Earth itself. They defined the meter as one ten-millionth of the distance from the North Pole to the equator, measured along the meridian that runs through Paris. And then, in the middle of a revolution and a war, they actually sent two astronomers, Delambre and Méchain, out to survey the arc of that meridian from Dunkirk on the English Channel down to Barcelona in Spain — a task that took them about six years and nearly broke both of them. The idea was breathtaking: authority would no longer descend from a throne. It would be read off of nature, and therefore belong to everyone equally. That is the Enlightenment in a single unit of length.

Now hold that thought, because America was having the very same impulse at almost exactly the same moment — and this is where the two countries start to rhyme, and then diverge. In seventeen eighty-four, Thomas Jefferson sat down and argued that the young United States should count its money in tens: a dollar, divided into tenths, and hundredths — cents. No shillings, no pence, no fractions a farmer couldn't calculate. And Congress agreed, in seventeen eighty-five and again in seventeen eighty-six. The United States became, as far as anyone can tell, the first nation on Earth to adopt a decimal currency — beating France to it. The same year, seventeen eighty-five, Congress passed the Land Ordinance, and here the rationalizing spirit becomes something you can see from an airplane. The government took the enormous territory northwest of the Ohio River and ruled a grid over it: townships six miles square, each cut into thirty-six sections of one square mile, six hundred and forty acres each. That relentless checkerboard you fly over between Ohio and Nebraska — that is the Enlightenment written on the ground. Reason, imposed on wilderness.

So both revolutions had the same dream: sweep away the inherited mess, and build on clean geometry instead. But watch what each one did with power, because this is the sharpest contrast between them, and it is, I'd argue, the whole ballgame. France took the map of itself — the ancient provinces, Provence, Burgundy, Brittany, Normandy, places that had been kingdoms and duchies with their own laws and languages and pride — and it abolished them. In seventeen ninety it carved the country into about eighty-three new units called departments, sized so that you could ride from any point to the local capital in a single day, and — this is the deliberate part — named not for the old regions but for rivers and mountains. The Seine. The Rhône. The Alps. Neutral geography, chosen precisely to erase the memory of Burgundy and Brittany, to dissolve the old loyalties into one undifferentiated nation looking to Paris. Power flowed to the center, and the center reached down into every valley.

America, with the same grid-making impulse, did the opposite with power. It kept it distributed. The states were not dissolved into rational districts named for rivers; they kept their borders, their identities, their own laws, and a Constitution written to hold them in tension with the center rather than to abolish them. And that is the enduring difference between these two children of the Enlightenment. France answered the great question — where does authority come from — with one rational will, concentrated. America answered it with authority split, checked, and scattered on purpose. Two nations, same philosophy, opposite architecture.

And now the best story, the one that shows you exactly where the rationalizing dream hits its limit. Because the revolutionaries did not stop at money and land and maps. In seventeen ninety-three they came for time itself. They introduced a Republican Calendar. Twelve months of exactly thirty days each — with beautiful new names drawn from the seasons, so that autumn had a month meaning roughly "the misty month," and there was a hot summer month whose name gives us the word for the coup that eventually toppled the whole revolution. Each month was cut into three ten-day weeks, called décades, with five or six leftover festival days at the year's end to make the sum come out right. And the count of years started over: this was Year One of the Republic. It was, on paper, gorgeous and perfectly logical.

And it failed. Completely. Napoleon abolished it, and France went back to the old Gregorian calendar with the first of January, eighteen hundred six. Why did the meter survive for two hundred years and the calendar die in twelve? Here is my reading, and I'll flag it as interpretation rather than settled fact. A ten-day week means you work nine days and rest one, instead of six and one. The seven-day rhythm was woven into the church, into the market, into the body's sense of a week — and you cannot legislate that away by decree. The meter measured the world. The calendar tried to reorganize the felt texture of ordinary life, and ordinary life pushed back and won.

And that, I think, is the quiet lesson underneath all of it, the one to carry as you walk. The Revolution changed the measure of everything and the map of everything and it nearly changed the shape of the week. But the old provinces it tried to erase are exactly the names on your menu tonight — a Burgundy on the wine list, a Provençal daube, a Norman butter, a Breton crêpe. The departments won on the government's map. The provinces won at the table. Kings fall, revolutions legislate, astronomers survey the meridian — and underneath all of it, people keep cooking the food of a place that officially no longer exists.
