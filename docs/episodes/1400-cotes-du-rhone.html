Côtes du Rhône — A River You Can Drink

Tournon (Hermitage / wine excursion) · Monday, July 13, 2026 · 08:16

Stand on the deck this morning and look across the river. There, on the far bank at Tain-l'Hermitage, directly opposite our mooring at Tournon, is a single steep hill, maybe six hundred feet of it, rising off the valley floor like the back of a whale, and it is combed top to bottom with terraced vineyards, little walled beds of vines climbing the granite in ribbons. That hill has a small stone chapel near its crown. And that hill, that one modest hump of rock and vine, is quite possibly the most famous single vineyard slope in France. It is called Hermitage. And the wine it makes is the reason I want to talk today about the whole idea we have been circling all week, the idea the French call terroir, the notion that a place, a specific patch of ground, can be tasted.

Let me give you the one big structural fact that will make this entire river valley suddenly legible, because once you have it, every wine list from here to Avignon will organize itself in your head. The Rhône wine country splits cleanly into two halves, a north and a south, and they are almost like two different countries. Up here in the north, where we are right now, the slopes are steep and the bedrock is granite, and the red wine is made from essentially one grape: Syrah. That is it. The great northern reds, Hermitage right across the water, Crozes-Hermitage which is the larger appellation wrapped around it, Côte-Rôtie further up toward Vienne, and Cornas a little south, are all Syrah, and in the case of Cornas it is a hundred percent Syrah by law, nothing else permitted. These are the wines I think you will fall for, because they are exactly what you told me you love, smooth and earthy and big, dark and peppery and structured, with a note people always reach for the word bacon or smoke or crushed black olive to describe. The northern whites, meanwhile, come from three grapes with lovely names, Viognier, Marsanne, and Roussanne, and the Marsanne and Roussanne blends in particular, the white Hermitage and the white Crozes-Hermitage, are rich and rounded and textured, almost waxy, with that beeswax and quince and toasted-nut quality when they have some age. That is your buttery, balanced white, made right on the hill you are looking at.

Now the south, which we will sail toward later, is a different animal. It is warmer and flatter and broader, and its red wine is not one grape but a blend led by Grenache, that round, warm, generous, slightly sweet-fruited red grape, usually married to Syrah and Mourvèdre and sometimes a whole crowd of others. The most famous southern wine, Châteauneuf-du-Pape, is the extreme case: the appellation legally permits thirteen different grape varieties in the blend, eight red and five white, a fact that tells you everything about the two philosophies. The north is about purity, one grape, one slope, the voice of a single place sung solo. The south is about the blend, the chorus, the warm harmony of many grapes grown on stony ground under a stronger sun. And the workhorse wine, the one that says simply Côtes du Rhône on the label, is mostly this southern Grenache-led blend, the everyday red of the whole valley, and it is your friend, a smooth, earthy, uncomplicated pleasure for a Tuesday.

So here is what to actually order, because ideas are only worth anything if they end up in the glass. On this stop, drink the north. Ask for a Crozes-Hermitage red as your everyday choice, and if the excursion gives you the chance and the budget, taste a proper Hermitage, the real thing off that hill. Both are Syrah, both are what you called big and earthy. Then, and this is the one I most want you to try, get a white Crozes-Hermitage or a white Hermitage, the Marsanne-Roussanne blend, because it is precisely the smooth, buttery, balanced white you like and almost no American has ever tasted it. And for the pairings, which is where your kitchen and your palate meet: the northern Syrah wants grilled or peppered meat, it wants game, it wants a leg of lamb rubbed with the herbes de Provence you will smell in every market down here, rosemary and thyme and savory. The buttery whites want the river, the fish pulled from the water beneath you, or a chicken cooked in cream. And when we reach the south, the Grenache blends want a stew, a ratatouille, a smear of black olive tapenade on bread, the whole warm Mediterranean table you already cook from instinct.

Now, why is that hill across the river so revered, and why has it been for so long? This is where the history comes in, and it goes deep. The Romans planted vines on these slopes. When Rome held Lyon as its great regional capital just upriver, the wines of this stretch of the Rhône were already prized and traded, so we are talking about roughly two thousand years of continuous winemaking on that granite. That is the endurance I keep coming back to on these walks, the astonishing persistence of an everyday act, someone pruning a vine on that hill this spring the way someone pruned a vine on that hill under the emperors. As for the name, Hermitage, there is a wonderful story, and I have to flag it clearly as legend, because the documentary evidence is thin and the tale has surely been polished over the centuries. The story goes that in the early twelve hundreds a wounded knight named Gaspard de Stérimberg came home from the Albigensian Crusade, the brutal war against the heretics of southern France, and received permission to retire to that hill and live out his days as a hermit, an ermite, in a little chapel, tending vines in his solitude. From his hermitage, the legend says, the hill took its name. Believe as much of the knight as you like; the chapel is really up there, and you can see it from the deck.

The other thing protecting that name is more recent and very French, and it connects to everything we have said all week about the centralizing instinct of this country. The reason Hermitage means Hermitage, the reason a bottle labeled Crozes-Hermitage must come from a defined patch of ground and be made a defined way, is the Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée system, the AOC, the national framework that draws legal boundaries around these names and polices them. Hermitage was granted its status in the nineteen thirties, and the whole apparatus is really the French state doing to wine what it did to bread and to the meter, taking a thing of ordinary life and enshrining terroir itself as something worth legislating. It is the belief that place is real, made into law.

And here is the American thread, the genuine one, because you asked me to keep those honest. These northern grapes, Syrah and Viognier, and the southern ones, Grenache and Mourvèdre, are now grown very seriously back home, on the West Coast, in California and in Washington State. There is a whole loose movement of American winemakers who took up these varieties, starting in the nineteen eighties with a few characters in California, and they gave themselves the marvelous name the Rhône Rangers. So when you get home and you want to chase the memory of that big earthy Syrah or that buttery Marsanne, you can, made from cuttings whose lineage runs back to the hill you are staring at this morning. The vine crossed the ocean, the way so much has crossed between these two countries.

So take a good look before we cast off. What you see over there is granite and vine and a small chapel, an ordinary-looking hill. But two thousand years of people have decided that this exact ground tastes like something worth protecting, worth naming, worth crossing an ocean to replant. That is terroir. That is the whole argument in a glass. It is, quite literally, a river you can drink.
