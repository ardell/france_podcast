The Tongues of the South

The Midi — languages of the region · 09:42

Somewhere on this journey you are going to notice that the map is lying to you a little. It will tell you that you are in France, and you are, but the words painted on the signs at the edge of the villages will sometimes come in pairs. One name in French, and beneath it, in smaller letters, another name in a language that is not French at all. That second language is Occitan, and learning to see it is like putting on a pair of glasses that suddenly reveals an older country lying just underneath the one you thought you were traveling through. So let me give you those glasses, because the south of France is, if you know how to look and listen, one of the great linguistic borderlands of Europe.

Start with the deep fact, the one that explains everything else. Nearly every language spoken around here grew from the same root, and that root is Latin. When Rome governed this coast, ordinary people did not speak the polished Latin of Cicero. They spoke an everyday, workaday Latin, and when the empire fell and the roads went quiet and the regions drifted apart, that common Latin did not die. It splintered. In each valley, each province, it drifted a little differently, until after a few centuries the descendants of Latin could no longer quite understand one another. Listen to it happen. Take the Latin word for night, <fw lang="la" ipa="ˈnɔktem">noctem</fw>. In Italy that became <fw lang="it">notte</fw>. In Spain, <fw lang="es">noche</fw>. In the north of France it wore down to <fw lang="fr">nuit</fw>. And here in the Occitan south it became <fw lang="oc" ipa="ˈnɥɛtʃ">nuèch</fw>. One Roman word, <fw lang="la" ipa="ˈnɔktem">noctem</fw>, five children. French, Italian, Spanish, Portuguese, Catalan, Romanian, and the language of this region, Occitan, are all children of the same Roman parent, siblings who grew up in different houses. That is why, if you speak a little Italian, you will keep catching the edges of meaning on menus and signs down here. You are not imagining the resemblance. You are hearing cousins.

Now, the great division of France. In the Middle Ages the country split cleanly in two by language, and the dividing line ran roughly across the middle of what is now France. In the north they spoke the langue d'oïl, the language whose word for yes was <fw lang="fr">oïl</fw>, and that language, the language of Paris and the king, grew up to become modern French. In the south they spoke the langue d'oc, the language whose word for yes was simply <fw lang="fr">oc</fw>. Two halves of one country that could not even agree on how to say yes. And the southern tongue, the langue d'oc, is Occitan. The very name of the great region you are traveling toward, Languedoc, is nothing more than those two words fused together, the language of oc, turned into the name of a place. When you say Languedoc you are naming a whole civilization after the sound it used to say yes.

And this was not a peasant patois. In the twelve hundreds Occitan was one of the most prestigious literary languages in Europe. It was the language of the troubadours, the poet-musicians who essentially invented the Western idea of romantic love, the lover who longs and serves and idealizes from afar. Poets came from northern Italy and from Catalonia to write in Occitan, the way a later musician might feel he had to go to Vienna, or to New Orleans, to be taken seriously. This was a language with a golden age. And then it lost. The north conquered the south in the crusade against the Cathars, the crown swallowed the region, and over the following centuries Paris did something very deliberate. It made its own northern French the single language of the state, the courts, and above all the schools. There is a famous, brutal little sign that hung in southern schoolhouses well into the twentieth century, warning children that it was forbidden to spit on the floor and forbidden to speak Occitan, the mother tongue set exactly level with spitting. Generations of southern children were taught to be ashamed of the language their grandparents spoke at home. That is how a language dies. Not usually by massacre, but by being made to feel like something you wipe off your shoe.

And yet. Here is the part I find genuinely moving, and it is the deepest theme of this whole trip in miniature. The language would not fully die. In the eighteen fifties a young Provençal poet named Frédéric Mistral set out to save it, to prove Occitan could still carry great poetry. He spent twenty years writing a single epic poem in Provençal, and he compiled a vast dictionary of the language almost single-handedly. He founded a movement, the Félibrige, to defend and celebrate the southern tongue. And in nineteen hundred and four he won the Nobel Prize in Literature, for poetry written not in French but in Provençal Occitan. Imagine that. Europe's highest literary honor, given for work in a language the French state was busy trying to erase from its own schoolrooms. There is your recurring theme, standing right in front of you: the center tried to absorb the region completely, and the everyday life of the region, its songs, its words, its way of saying yes, refused to be absorbed.

So what can you actually notice, now, on the ground? More than you would expect. The clearest signs are literally signs. In Avignon and Aix and Arles and dozens of smaller towns, the street plaques and the town-entrance boards carry the old Provençal name beneath the French one, an official act of memory. Listen to the accent, too, because Occitan lives inside the way southerners speak French. The southern accent sounds the endings that a Parisian swallows. Take the word for bread, pain. A Parisian gives you a single clipped nasal, the word gone almost before it starts. A southerner leans on the tail of it, letting the ending ring out so the one syllable stretches toward two, a faint hummed g hanging in the air where the northerner gives you only silence. Do the same with the word for wine, vin, or the number twenty, vingt, and you hear the same thing, the north clipping the sound off short, the south ringing it out. It can sound, to a northern French ear, almost like singing, and that little sung ending is Occitan grammar breathing underneath the French, the older language shaping the mouth that now speaks the newer one. And the language is loudest of all in the place-names and the food. When you see the word mas for a farmhouse, or eat a daube, the slow Provençal beef stew, you are speaking Occitan without knowing it. And when you sit down to the great dish of Marseille, bouillabaisse, its very name is Occitan, built from the words for to boil and to lower, boil it up and turn it down, the cooking instruction fossilized into the name of the dish.

Now widen the lens, because this coast is a whole crossroads of tongues, not just two. Follow it east toward Italy and the languages blur into one another the way the border itself has wandered back and forth. The city of Nice only became French in eighteen sixty, and it has its own speech, Nissart, a branch of Occitan flavored heavily by Italian, and you will still find street signs in the old town written in it. Push right up to the Italian frontier, to Menton and the mountain valleys behind it, and you find pockets of a Ligurian speech, the tongue of Genoa, spilling across the modern border because languages have never cared much for the lines that states draw. And go the other way, west and south toward Spain, and around the city of Perpignan you cross into a land where the second language on the signs is not Occitan at all but Catalan, the language of Barcelona, because that corner of France is really the northern edge of the Catalan world, French by treaty but Catalan by blood and by kitchen.

Here is the American parallel, and I offer it as a genuine echo rather than an exact match. The United States tells itself a story of one national language, English, and yet the map underneath is thick with other tongues that the official story tends to skip. The Spanish of the entire Southwest, older in places than English. The French of Louisiana. The hundreds of Native languages the country spent a century trying to school out of children, in boarding schools with signs on the wall that would have been perfectly familiar to a child in nineteenth-century Provence. Every nation-state does this. It picks one language, calls it the national one, and quietly leans on all the others. And everywhere, the others hang on longer than anyone expects, in the place-names, in the kitchen, in the accent, in the stubborn old word for yes.

So as the signs go by, read the second line. That smaller name underneath is not decoration. It is the receipt for a conquest, and also the proof that the conquest was never quite complete.
