The Cure and the Light

The journey south — the Midi · 08:17

As the train runs south and the light through the window begins to change, sharpen, get harder and cleaner and more golden, it is worth asking a simple question. Who else came this way, and why? Because you are not the first person to travel south in France looking for something. People have been doing it for two and a half centuries, and the reasons they gave are a small history of what human beings have wanted from the world. They came for their health. They came for the light. They came to escape a war, or a scandal, or a winter, or themselves. And the strange thing is that almost none of them came for the reasons we go now. The south of France as a place of pleasure, of sun and wine and long lunches, is a fairly recent invention, and it was invented, oddly enough, by sick people.

Start with the man who arguably started it all, a Scottish novelist named Tobias Smollett. In 1763 Smollett was in a bad way. He was in his early forties, asthmatic and probably tubercular, worn down by literary feuds and shattered by the death of his only child, a daughter of fifteen. He was, in the language of the time, an invalid, and like many invalids he was sent south in the hope that a warmer, drier climate might do for his lungs what the doctors could not. He landed in Nice, which at the time was not even French, it belonged to the Kingdom of Sardinia, and he stayed for about eighteen months, and he was, by temperament, a magnificent complainer. He found fault with the food, the people, the roads, the innkeepers. But he also kept careful notes on the weather, the temperature, the sea, and in 1766 he published a book called Travels through France and Italy, and it became a bestseller.

And here is the consequence that Smollett never intended. His grumbling, meticulous account of the mild winters of Nice put the idea into the heads of the British upper classes that the Mediterranean coast was a place where a person with weak lungs might survive the winter that would kill them at home. The industrial cities of England were foul with coal smoke. Tuberculosis was everywhere, one of the great killers of the age, and no one could cure it, so the only thing medicine could offer was flight, a change of air. And the south of France had the air. So they came. First a trickle of consumptive aristocrats and their families, then, once the railway arrived in the 1860s and cut the journey from London from two weeks to barely thirty hours, a flood. Whole towns reorganized themselves around the seasonal arrival of wealthy invalids. Menton, just along the coast, built its entire reputation on the promise that its climate could ease, even cure, the disease that was consuming its visitors. The English built churches, laid out gardens, put up villas, and gave the coast the shape it still has. It is one of history's quiet ironies that the glittering Riviera, the Riviera of casinos and yachts and film festivals, began as an open-air hospital.

Now hold onto that word, climate, because it is doing something important. For the invalids, the south was medicine. But there was a second wave of travelers coming south for a reason that sounds similar and is actually completely different. They came not for the climate but for the light. And these were the painters.

You already know one of them, because he is why you will stop in Arles. Vincent van Gogh came south to Arles in 1888, and although his health was part of it, he was chasing something else, a quality of light he could not find in the gray north. He wrote about it constantly, the way the Provençal sun did not soften and diffuse everything the way northern light does, but instead sharpened it, deepened every color, threw hard clean shadows, made the yellows more yellow and the blues more blue. For a painter, that was not comfort. That was raw material. And he was not alone. Renoir, crippled with arthritis, moved down to the coast to ease his joints and stayed for the color. Matisse came to Nice in 1917 to recover from bronchitis and never really left, painting rooms full of that southern light for the rest of his life. The invalids and the painters were often looking at the very same sunshine. But one group saw a cure for the body, and the other saw a revolution in how to see. That is the doubleness of the south. The same light that was prescribed as medicine was also, for the right eyes, an argument about art.

There is a third kind of traveler, and this one an American will recognize instantly. These are the people who came south not to heal and not to paint, but to escape, and to reinvent themselves cheaply and beautifully far from home. After the First World War, a whole generation of Americans discovered that the franc was weak and the living was easy and the coast was gorgeous, and they came in droves. Scott and Zelda Fitzgerald were among them, drifting along the Riviera in the 1920s, and Fitzgerald poured that world, its expatriates, its golden mornings and its private ruin, into his novel Tender Is the Night. This was the Lost Generation at play, Americans who found in the south of France a stage on which to be someone other than who they had been at home. And there is a deep continuity here with the invalids and the painters, because all of them, the consumptive lord, the half-mad painter, the runaway American novelist, were doing the same fundamental thing. They were coming south because the south let you be a different person than the north would allow. It was where you went to get well, or to see clearly, or to start over.

And I want to draw out the American thread, because it is genuinely revealing. Why did the south of France become, for Americans especially, this dreamed-of place of escape and reinvention? Part of it is simple economics and the exchange rate. But part of it is something older in the American character. Americans have always believed, almost as a matter of national faith, that a person can go somewhere and become new. That is the logic of the frontier, of Gatsby remaking himself, of the westward migration, the idea that the self is not fixed by where you were born but can be shed and redrawn in a new place. And when that instinct went looking for a stage in Europe, it found the south of France, a landscape that already had, from the invalids and the painters, a reputation as a place of transformation. The French came south to survive the winter. The Americans came south to become someone else. Same coast, different dream.

But here is the thing I keep returning to, and it is the quiet point underneath all of this. Every one of these waves of famous visitors, the aristocrats, the artists, the expatriates, was passing through a place where people already lived, and had lived, and would go on living, long after the visitors packed up and went home. While the consumptive lords were dying elegantly in their villas and the painters were arguing about light and the Fitzgeralds were drinking their way toward tragedy, the people of Provence and the Rhône valley were doing what they had always done. Pressing olives. Tending vines. Hauling fish. Making the same bread and the same stews their grandparents made. The glamorous visitors are the part of the story that gets written down, the names in the guidebooks, the plaques on the villas. But they were guests. The enduring thing was never the famous invalid or the celebrated painter. It was the ordinary daily life of the place that drew them, the life that was here before any of them arrived, and is here still, out the window, as your train runs south into the light they all came looking for.
