The Arrow from Paris — The TGV and the Shape of France

TGV, Paris Gare de Lyon to Arles · Thursday, July 9, 2026 · 07:49

You are standing under the great vaulted hall of the Gare de Lyon in Paris, and if you have a few minutes before you board, look up. There is a restaurant here called Le Train Bleu, all gilded plaster and painted ceilings, named for the old luxury sleeper that carried the wealthy down to the Riviera. It is a room built to make the act of leaving Paris feel like an occasion. And that is the right frame of mind for what you are about to do, because the train you are about to take, the TGV, is not just a fast way to get to Arles. It is one of the purest expressions of how France thinks about itself.

The letters stand for train à grande vitesse, high-speed train, and the story begins in the nineteen-sixties, when the French were flirting with a very different idea. The first experiments used gas turbines, jet-engine technology borrowed from aviation, and there is something wonderfully of-its-moment about that, the age of Concorde, when the future was supposed to arrive with a roar. But the oil shock of the early seventies made burning kerosene look foolish, and France had something better: cheap, abundant electricity from its rapidly expanding fleet of nuclear reactors. So the TGV became an electric train, and in that single decision you can already see the French state doing what it does best, marshaling national resources toward a single grand project.

The first line opened on the twenty-seventh of September, nineteen eighty-one, running from this very station down to Lyon, which is where your river cruise will eventually end. Before it opened, in a test run that February, a prototype hit three hundred and eighty kilometers an hour, well over two hundred miles an hour, faster than any train had gone on conventional rails. In everyday service they held it back, but the message was sent. And the French kept pushing. In two thousand and seven, on a specially prepared line east toward Strasbourg, a stripped-down TGV reached five hundred and seventy-four point eight kilometers an hour. That is three hundred and fifty-seven miles an hour, on steel wheels, on ordinary steel rail. It remains the world record for a conventional train, and the French will tell you about it whether you ask or not.

Here is what is genuinely clever about the machine under you. A TGV is not one long snake of identical cars. At each end sits a power car, a dedicated locomotive full of motors, and the passenger coaches ride between them. And those coaches share their wheels. Instead of each car sitting on its own set of bogies, the trucks that hold the wheels, adjacent cars rest on a single shared bogie between them, an arrangement called articulation. This does something subtle and important. It means the cars cannot jackknife independently in a derailment; the train stays in line, like a string of beads that cannot fold. It lowers the weight, it smooths the ride, and it is one of the quiet reasons the TGV has a safety record that, for a machine moving this fast, is close to miraculous. The train you are likely on is a Duplex, double-decker, and if you can, take a seat on the upper level. Each set is about two hundred meters long, carries somewhere north of five hundred passengers, and they often couple two together into a single quarter-mile-long train that splits at a junction, half going one way, half the other, like a river dividing.

Walk the train once it is moving and you will find the grammar of French travel laid out car by car. There is first class and second class, and the difference is real but not obscene, a bit more room, a bit more quiet. Somewhere in the middle is the bar car, the voiture-bar, where you can get a coffee, a sandwich, a small bottle of wine, and stand at a counter watching the countryside blur past at a speed that makes the near fields dissolve into green while the distant hills drift by with dignity. It is a good place to be. Buy a coffee there in the first hour and you will understand something about the trip: the everyday ritual, the small pleasure of a drink in hand, riding inside this monument of engineering. The grand and the humble, side by side. That contrast is going to follow us all the way down the Rhône.

But the deepest thing about the TGV is not the speed or the engineering. It is the map. Look at a diagram of the French high-speed network and you will see lines radiating out from Paris like spokes from a hub, to Lyon and Marseille, to Lille and the Channel, to Bordeaux, to Strasbourg. Paris is the center, and everywhere else is measured by its distance from the center. This is not an accident of geography. It is a deliberate continuation of a project that is centuries old. When Louis the Fourteenth built Versailles and pulled the nobility in around himself, when the Revolution swept away the old provinces and redrew France into tidy departments administered from the capital, when Napoleon built the roads, the logic was always the same: power flows to the center, and the center reaches out to bind the territory together. The TGV is that same impulse, now running at three hundred kilometers an hour. To get from one provincial city to another, it is often fastest to go through Paris. The nation is quite literally organized around its capital, and you feel it in the timetable.

Now here is where I want to draw the line across the Atlantic, because this is the kind of comparison that tells you something about both countries. The United States, which is richer than France and has cities begging for connection, has essentially no high-speed rail. And the reasons are not only about distance or money. They are about how the two countries answer the question of where authority lives. France could build the TGV because a strong central state could decide, on behalf of the whole nation, to seize the land, fund the project, and run the railroad, and the citizens broadly accepted that this was a legitimate thing for the state to do. America was founded on a suspicion of exactly that kind of power. Authority in the American system is deliberately scattered, among the states, among private interests, among a thousand veto points, and a project that needs to cross many hands and many jurisdictions tends to die in the crossing. The French built a national arrow. The Americans built an interstate highway system and an airline network, distributed and privately flown, which is its own kind of answer. Neither is simply right. They are two different settlements of the same old question, the one we will keep circling back to on this trip: who gets to decide, and on whose behalf.

And yet, for all that centralizing power, notice what the train is actually doing for you. It is carrying you away from the center. In a little under four hours it will set you down in Arles, a town that was already old when Paris was a muddy village, a place that answered to Rome long before it answered to any French king. The TGV is Paris's arrow, but it is delivering you to a Provence that has its own light, its own accent, its own way of cooking with olive oil and garlic and the herbs that grow wild on the hillsides, a daily life that was here before the railroad and will be here long after this particular train is scrapped. That is the thing to hold onto as the fields go by. The state builds its monuments and draws its maps, and they matter enormously. But the market will still open in Arles on Saturday morning, the way it has for two thousand years. Kings and republics and railways come and go. The everyday endures. Enjoy the ride down.
